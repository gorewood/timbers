Spent an afternoon doing the kind of work that never makes a changelog exciting but saves you from slow-burning annoyance: **housekeeping a Dolt-backed beads setup** after a version bump.

The main event was migrating to beads 0.59's hash-derived port scheme. Prior to this, the dolt server port was hardcoded — `3308` in our case, `3307` being the common default. The 0.59 approach derives a port from a hash, which means multiple beads on the same machine won't stomp each other. Elegant idea, but it means you have to **actively remove** your old hardcoded overrides or the whole collision-prevention mechanism is just decorative. Cleared `dolt_server_port` from `metadata.json`, nuked `dolt.port` from `config.yaml`, ran `bd dolt start`, and it landed on port **13517**. Then `bd hooks install --force` to get the hook scripts stamped with the new v0.59 markers.

After that, `bd dolt commit && bd dolt pull` to sync with the remote, and a rebase to merge the hardening branch into main. Rebase rewrote the SHAs for work that already had devlog entries — one of those small rebase-vs-merge tradeoffs where you trade a clean history for orphaned references. Covered it with a single catch-up entry and moved on.

The more satisfying fix came at the end. I noticed the `.beads/backup/` directory was tracked in git, and *every single `bd` operation* was generating noisy diffs — timestamps shifting, dolt commit hashes changing. Pure machine-local runtime state that had no business being versioned. A quick `git rm -r --cached .beads/backup/` and a `.gitignore` addition, and that entire class of noise just vanished. The kind of tiny cleanup that makes `git status` trustworthy again.

> It's always the runtime state that sneaks into version control. You track it once during initial setup, forget about it, and then spend weeks wondering why your diffs are always dirty.

Also added `dolt-monitor.pid.lock` to `.gitignore` — same spirit, different file. PID locks are the textbook example of "should never have been tracked."

Nothing flashy here. Just the steady work of making a tool setup actually *stay clean* between operations instead of accumulating cruft that you learn to ignore until you can't.
